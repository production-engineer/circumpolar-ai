Models the financial value of early permafrost detection using a Monte Carlo simulation. Each run simulates 500 independent futures — damage events are discrete shocks, not smooth averages. The risk distribution shows the full range of outcomes so you can see both the typical case and the tail risk.

▶ How this works
Scenario simulation: We run 500 simulated futures. In each year of each scenario, a random draw determines whether a damage event occurs — it's binary, either it happens or it doesn't, based on the annual chance you set. If it does, the unmonitored building pays the full repair cost; the ThawRisk building pays the smaller early-intervention cost instead. Cumulative savings = total unmonitored cost − total ThawRisk cost to date. The savings chart shows the mean cumulative savings across all scenarios. The risk distribution shows the full range of average annual costs — a tool insurers and lenders use to price risk.
Each scenario, each year:
damage_event = yes/no at probability p
unmonitored cost += damage_event × repair_cost
ThawRisk cost += annual_fee + damage_event × intervention_cost

Cumulative savings(yr) = unmonitored cost − ThawRisk cost
Risk chart: sort final avg. annual costs → % chance of exceeding each value

Assumptions: In recurring mode, damage events are independent year to year. In one-time mode, once an event fires and mitigation is applied, no further events occur — the risk is resolved. ThawRisk catches events early enough to use the lower intervention cost. No inflation adjustment applied.

Not included: lost revenue during repairs, insurance savings, and the risk of damage going undetected for multiple years (which makes costs compound). All of these make ThawRisk look better than shown.
